Seasonal ARIMA models

  • Seasonality in a time series is a regular pattern of changes that repeats over \(m\) time periods, where \(m\) defines the number of time periods until the pattern repeats again.
    • For monthly data m=12.
    • For quarterly data m=4.
  • A seasonal ARIMA model uses seasonal AR and MA terms to make a predict.
    • using data values and errors at times with lags that are multiples of \(m\) (the span of the seasonality).
  • For instance, if we were selling cooling fans we might predict this Augusts sales using last Augusts sales.
    • With monthly data (and m = 12), a seasonal first order autoregressive model would use to make a predict.
    • If we would predict this Augusts values from the past two Augusts, a seasonal second order autoregressive model would use to make a predict.

Data Example

  • Suppose we have quarterly European retail trade data from 1996 to 2011.

  • For such a data set, we will describe the seasonal ARIMA modelling procedure. Specifically, we will perform data transformation (only if needed), model identification, model selection, diagnostic checking, parameter estimation, and forecast the next two years (24 observations).

  • We will follow the logic: (1) data visualization; (2) the identification of the model to be fit together with our rational based upon the appropriate plots and outputs;(3) estimation of the model parameters and interpretation of significance of the parameters; (4) diagnostic checking on residuals and possible revision to your model choice; (5) forecasts for the period in question.

Step 2: Model Identification

  • We use the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test (ADF) to determine its stationarity.

  • The null hypothesis for the test is that the data is non-stationary.

  • A p-value of ADF (0.99) is greater than 0.05. Hence, we fail to reject the null hypothesis for this test.

library(fpp2)

Step 4: Diagnostic Check (contd)

  • We use Ljung-Box test:
Box.test(resid(fit), lag = 10, type = "Ljung-Box")
## 
##  Box-Ljung test
## 
## data:  resid(fit)
## X-squared = 3.7311, df = 10, p-value = 0.9587

which fails to reject the null hypothesis.

  • We fail to reject the null hypothesis \(H_{0}\). Thus, we can conclude that the residuals are NOT distinguishable from a white noise series.

  • Note: Ljung-Box test

    • In general, the Ljung-Box test (a test of autocorrelation of the residuals) is defined as:
      • \(H_{0}\) (null hypothesis): the autocorrelations come from a white noise series.
      • \(H_{a}\) (alternative hypothesis): the autocorrelations do not come from a white noise series.

  • Hence, our final model is the ARIMA(0,1,3)(0,1,1)[4]:\((1-B^4)(1-B)y_t\)=0.26 \(\varepsilon_{t-1}+0.37\varepsilon_{t-2}+0.42\varepsilon_{t-3}-0.67 \varepsilon_{t-4}+\varepsilon_{t}\), where \(\varepsilon_t\sim(0, \sigma^2)\).

  • Note:

    • Autoregressive Moving Average models (ARMA):
      • \(y_{t}=c+\phi_{1}y_{t-1}+\cdots+\phi_{p}y_{t-p}+\theta_{1}\varepsilon_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_{q}\varepsilon_{t -q}+\varepsilon_{t}\), where \(\varepsilon_t\) is white noise.
    • Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average models (ARIMA):
      • Combine ARMA model with differencing.
      • \((1-B)^d y_t\) follows an ARMA model.
      • A first difference can be written as \(y'_{t} = y_{t} - y_{t-1} = y_t - By_{t} = (1 - B)y_{t}\).

Backup

  • The autocorrelation function (ACF) measures the linear relationship between lagged values of a time series.
    • Visualizes how much the most recent value of the series is correlated with past values of the series (lags).
    • If the data has \(\color{blue}{\text{a trend}}\), then the autocorrelations for small lags tend to be positive and large because observations nearby in time are also nearby in size.
    • If the data are \(\color{blue}{\text{seasonal}}\), then the autocorrelations will be larger for seasonal lags at multiples of seasonal frequency than other lags.
  • The partial autocorrelation function (PACF) measures the linear relationship between the correlations of the residuals .
    • Visualizes whether certain lags are good for modeling or not; useful for data with a seasonal pattern.
    • Removes dependence of lags on other lags by using the correlations of the residuals.
